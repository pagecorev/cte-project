Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE)

Raising Awareness for Concussion Protection

What is CTE?

Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE) is an uncommon brain disease caused by repeated head trauma. Head trauma refers to any injury related to the head, scalp, or brain. This injury can affect both motor (both gross and fine) and cognitive (thinking) abilities and can develop over many years.

Historically called dementia pugilistica, it was first noted in boxers in the early 20th century. Symptoms include memory loss, mood changes, and impaired coordination.

The brain imaging shown here highlights areas affected by repeated trauma.

Symptoms

CTE symptoms develop slowly and can include confusion, memory loss, impaired judgment, depression, and many more. Sub-concussive impacts, which are impacts that do not have immediate symptoms such as confusion or dizziness, can also contribute to the likeliness of getting the disease.

While concussions are often the main focus, repeated smaller impacts over a long period of time, are just as dangerous as harder impacts.

Mood and emotional changes are often early warning signs of CTE.

Other Notes

Sub-concussive impacts: Injuries that do not cause immediate symptoms such as dizziness or confusion, but can cause damage to the brain over time.

Prevention of CTE: Proper safety equipment, limiting contact exposure, and awareness are very important to prevent CTE, more prominently in sports and high-risk activities such as DUI.
