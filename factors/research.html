Research & Lawsuits

Research in (CTE) has grown very fast over recent decades. Repeated head impacts, even without concussions (sub-concussive impacts), can slowly damage the brain, meaning they could be even more dangerous than normal impacts if disregarded and not delt with properly.

Thousands of former NFL players filed lawsuits about concussions and brain injury risks.

In 2013, the NFL settled an agreement for 765 million dollars, with over 4,000 former players, providing medical support for memory loss, depression, and other conditions. Some argued the league unfairly profited from dangerous gameplay, while completely disregarding player saftey.

CTE research expanded to hockey after the deaths of former NHL players.

New safety rules were put in place in order to protect players in the NFL and NHL (National Hockey League): stricter concussion protocols, limits on dangerous hits, better equipment, and many more were also put in place to protect players. There is still research going on for less "harmful" sports such as soccer and baseball.

Overall, research and lawsuits have forced sports organizations to take player safety more seriously and improve standards for athletes.

Practice drills and rules are modified to reduce cumulative head impacts.
